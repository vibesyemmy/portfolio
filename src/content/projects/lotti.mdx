## 3,000+ tasks tracked · AI-powered · iOS + Desktop · $5K, 3 milestones, fully delivered

---

## The Promise

Lotti is named after Lieselotte, Matthias's late grandmother. She kept meticulous paper records of her life and, even in her final years, could recall extraordinary detail from her own organized notes. When Matthias showed her an early prototype in 2018, she made him promise to turn it into something real.

Seven years later, Matthias had a technically complete product running in TestFlight, tracking thousands of his own tasks. But his psychiatrist friend kept asking the same question: "Why isn't this out yet?"

The honest answer: it needed design. A technical founder had built a functioning AI productivity app, but three overlapping organizational systems confused even him. The flagship timeline screen didn't reflect how work actually happens. The AI configuration had 20+ raw checkboxes per category. There was no onboarding. The product worked, but nobody who wasn't the founder would survive the first five minutes.

I was brought in as a freelance senior designer from Lagos, Nigeria, working remotely with a first-time client in Hamburg, Germany. Cold referral. No existing relationship. Three months, three milestones, $5,000.

---

## What Was Actually Broken

I started with a comprehensive UI audit across every screen. The findings clustered into three problems:

**The information architecture was fighting itself.** Categories, Labels, and Tags all existed as overlapping organizational systems. The founder himself couldn't explain when to use which. Categories were required for task creation. Labels were optional status markers. Tags were freeform hashtags. Three taxonomies, one confused user.

**The timeline told the wrong story.** My Daily, the flagship screen, showed planned time blocks as rigid containers. Real work rarely fits inside planned blocks. Tasks started early, ran late, got interrupted, resumed hours later. The interface treated every deviation as an error state, when it was actually the normal state.

**The AI was powerful but inaccessible.** Lotti had genuinely useful AI features: task summaries, conversational agent, automatic transcription, screenshot analysis. But the configuration screen was a wall of 20+ raw checkboxes with no presets, no progressive disclosure, and no explanation of what enabling each checkbox would actually do.

The product had depth. It just had no surface.

## Making It Make Sense

### Categories, Labels, and Tags: three systems, three jobs

The fix wasn't merging them. It was giving each a clear, non-overlapping job:

- **Categories** (required): the primary organizing structure. "Lotti Works," "Personal," "Driving." Each carries its own AI settings, color, and icon. You pick one, always.
- **Labels** (optional, multiple): workflow status markers scoped to a category. "Bug," "Urgent," "Waiting." Structured, finite, purposeful.
- **Tags** (optional, multiple, freeform): emergent taxonomy. #topic, @person, #story-thread. Grows organically as the user works.

Three systems became three distinct tools. The founder stopped confusing them. New users would never need to learn the difference because the difference was now obvious from the interaction design.

### My Daily: time blocks as context, not constraints

The flagship redesign. Instead of time blocks as rigid containers that tasks must fit inside, I designed them as background context:

- Time blocks render as vertical colored bars showing planned focus periods
- Task cards render as horizontal cards at their actual work time
- Tasks inside their matching block show at full opacity. Tasks worked on outside their planned block fade to 50–60%, a deliberate visual cue that says "this wasn't the plan" without shaming, blocking, or erroring
- Multi-session tasks (interrupted and resumed work) are visually linked with curved connecting lines. The first card shows full task info with a session count. Continuation cards show minimal info

This was the core design philosophy: **honest tracking, not aspirational tracking.** Most productivity tools assume you'll work in unbroken focus sessions. Lotti assumes you'll get interrupted, and it visualizes that honestly rather than punishing it.

### AI: from checkbox wall to progressive disclosure

The AI configuration went from 20+ raw checkboxes to a tiered setup:

- **Quick presets** at the top: Minimal, Balanced, Maximum. One tap.
- **Custom** below, collapsed by default, with grouped toggles under clear headings
- Each toggle explained in plain language: what it does, when you'd want it on

The AI was no less powerful. It was just no longer terrifying.

## Three Ways to Show a Health Score

One of the more interesting design problems was a single project health indicator. How do you communicate "is this task on track?" at a glance?

I designed and comparatively tested three distinct directions:

1. **Action-focused** (final direction): a circular progress ring around the score, a status callout, and a single blocker action. One thing to fix, clearly named.
2. **Metric dashboard**: large score plus a sparkline trend, a 2×2 status grid, and an action banner. More data, more cognitive load.
3. **Status-first**: a large top banner leading with the blocker action, with the score demoted to secondary info. Good for urgency, weaker for at-a-glance status.

I delivered these as an interactive side-by-side HTML comparison so Matthias could evaluate all three with live data before committing. He picked the action-focused direction. The comparison artifact meant the decision took one call instead of three rounds of Figma revisions.

## The Cross-Continental Piece

This project happened between Lagos and Hamburg. Cold referral from a mutual contact. First-time client. Remote throughout.

What made it work:

- **Structured milestones with clear deliverables** reduced the risk of a first engagement. Three milestones, each with defined outputs, each with payment attached.
- **The Yoruba language support** wasn't a feature request, it was a natural outcome of the team composition. A German founder and a Nigerian designer building together produced a product that ships in Yoruba, a genuine differentiator in the productivity space.
- **Two cultural lenses on data privacy** shaped the AI design: GDPR-conscious European expectations alongside mobile-first, connectivity-conscious thinking from the Nigerian market.

The product is better because the team wasn't homogeneous.

## What I'd Do Differently

**Push back harder on the scope-to-budget ratio.** The original internal pricing exercise landed between $6,000 and $10,000. I negotiated down to $5,000 for the first engagement to build the relationship. The work delivered was closer to the higher end. First-client discounting is common, but this one was steep for the depth of output.

**Fight for earlier access to real user data.** Matthias had 3,000+ tasks of his own usage data, which was invaluable for understanding the product's actual behavior. But it was also a sample size of one. Even three external beta testers during the audit phase would have surfaced issues the founder had learned to work around.

**Prototype the Health Score earlier.** The three-direction comparison was effective, but I built it late in the engagement. If I'd prototyped it during Phase 1, the decision would have been baked into the Phase 2 timeline redesign rather than retrofitted.

---

Lotti taught me something I already suspected: a technically complete product is not a launch-ready product. The gap between "it works" and "people can use it" is design. Not decoration. The structural kind that makes three overlapping taxonomies feel like one clear system, and turns a wall of checkboxes into a single tap.
