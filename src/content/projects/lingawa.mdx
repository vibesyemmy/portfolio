## 5.0★ Play Store · 5.0★ App Store · 4.9 Trustpilot (200+ reviews) · 6,000+ learners · $1.1M raised · Solo designer

---

## The Problem

Language learning apps are a solved problem, if you're learning French from English. But if you're a second-generation Nigerian in London trying to reconnect with Yoruba, the apps don't understand you. They don't know your auntie already taught you greetings. They don't know you can understand more than you can speak. They certainly don't have exercises for tonal pronunciation.

Lingawa set out to build for this learner: the diaspora user who carries fragmented knowledge, cultural context, and a different kind of motivation. Not "learn a language for travel," but "learn the language your grandmother speaks."

When I joined as the sole product designer, the company had a working web platform but no mobile presence. No mobile design system. No app brand identity. The goal: take a web-only learning platform and bring it to iOS and Android, with a design system that could serve both the existing web experience and the new mobile app.

Two years later, the app holds a 5.0 rating on both stores. The company raised $1.1 million. And I designed every screen.

---

## The Bet

Most language apps compete on content volume: thousands of lessons, endless vocab lists, gamification loops designed to maximize session time. That wasn't our play.

We bet on **breadth of modality over depth of content**. Instead of one exercise type repeated across 500 lessons, we built seven distinct ways to engage with the language:

- **Lesson slides** for structured instruction
- **Vocab drills** for spaced repetition
- **Matching games** for rapid recall
- **Dialogue practice** for conversational fluency
- **Sentence construction** with two difficulty tiers
- **Story-time reader** for immersive comprehension
- **Practice hub** tying it all together

The logic: a diaspora learner's weak spot isn't vocabulary. It's the gap between understanding and speaking. You know "Ẹ káàárọ̀" means good morning. But can you use it naturally in a sentence? Can you respond when someone says it faster than you expected? Different modalities target different gaps.

The second bet: **a solo designer with a design system can outpace a team without one.** Every screen, every component, every interaction state had to be consistent because there was nobody to double-check my work. The design system wasn't a nice-to-have. It was survival.

## The Process

### Starting from zero

I joined Lingawa with a web platform in production but nothing for mobile. No mobile design tokens. No mobile component library. No user research on how learners would use the product on a phone. The engineering team was small and needed to start building immediately, which meant the design system had to ship in parallel with the product screens, not before them.

I built the system incrementally: design a screen, extract the reusable parts, componentize them, repeat. After the first three screens, I had buttons, inputs, and a color system. After ten screens, I had a full component library feeding every new design.

### Learning from learners

We interviewed diaspora learners across Nigeria, the UK, and the US. The pattern that emerged: **most learners could understand more than they could speak.** Listening comprehension outpaced speaking ability by a wide margin. Traditional apps treated them as complete beginners, which felt patronizing.

This insight shaped the exercise design. Dialogue practice and story-time reader leaned into comprehension strength. Sentence construction bridged the gap between understanding and producing. Vocab drills weren't about learning new words, they were about activating passive vocabulary.

### The mascot

Early on, I collaborated on creating a brand mascot, a character that appeared throughout the app as a guide, a coach, and sometimes comic relief. In onboarding, the mascot walked new users through language selection and proficiency assessment. During exercises, it celebrated streaks and softened error states. For returning users, it greeted them by name.

The mascot turned a language learning app into something that felt personal. Users weren't practicing vocabulary into the void. They had a companion.

### Seven modalities, one system

Building seven distinct exercise types while maintaining visual consistency was the central design challenge. A matching game needs large tappable cards and rapid feedback. A dialogue screen needs a chat-like interface with speaker attribution. A story reader needs comfortable long-form typography. Vocab drills need focused repetition with minimal chrome.

The design system made this possible. Shared tokens for color, spacing, and typography meant every modality felt like the same product, even when the layouts differed completely. Components like progress indicators, avatars, and navigation bars worked across every screen. When I designed the Kids mode, a parallel experience with simplified navigation and larger touch targets, I reused 70% of the existing components.

### The rebrand

Midway through, the company rebranded. New name, new visual identity, new brand voice. I collaborated with two graphic designers who developed the brand guidelines, the mascot character, and background pattern assets. The marketing team defined the new brand voice and messaging.

For a solo product designer, a rebrand typically means reworking every screen individually over weeks of pixel-pushing. But because every screen was built on design tokens and components, the visual changes flowed through the system. New colors, new typography scale, new logo. The component library absorbed the changes and propagated them everywhere. What still took a month of stakeholder reviews and iterations across the brand, marketing, and product teams would have taken three without the design system foundation.

## The Outcomes

### Quality at scale

The app launched on both Google Play Store and Apple App Store. One year post-launch:

- **Play Store:** 1,000+ downloads, 5.0★ rating
- **App Store:** 2,500+ downloads, 5.0★ rating
- **Trustpilot:** 4.9★ rating

These numbers are rare. A 5.0 on either store is exceptional. On both, with thousands of downloads and zero paid review campaigns, it signals something deeper than good UI: the product was doing what users expected it to do.

### Business impact

The $1.1 million raised during my tenure wasn't raised on a pitch deck alone. Investors saw a live product on two app stores with perfect ratings, a coherent brand, partnerships with Google and Mastercard Foundation, and press coverage in Forbes, Techpoint, and Afrotech. The design work was the visible layer of that credibility.

By launch, the platform had over 6,000 learners across the diaspora, a 4.9 TrustScore on Trustpilot with 200+ reviews, and a referral program driving organic growth.

### Design system velocity

- **40+ screens** designed across two major iterations (V1 and Iteration 2)
- **7 exercise modalities** plus onboarding, social, progress, and Kids mode
- **Rebrand** absorbed by token-level design system changes despite a month of cross-team stakeholder reviews
- **Kids mode** launched reusing 70% of existing components

## What I'd Do Differently

**Start the design system before the first screen.** Building the system incrementally worked, but it meant the earliest screens had inconsistent spacing and typography that needed rework after the tokens were finalized. Even three days of system-first work would have saved a week of retrofitting.

**User-test exercise modalities individually before building all seven.** We built broadly first and narrowed later. A leaner approach: prototype three modalities, test comprehension-vs-production balance, then build the remaining four. We might have discovered earlier that sentence construction advanced mode had low engagement and needed restructuring.

**Fight for engineering pairing earlier.** As the solo designer, I was often weeks ahead of the development team. Screens sat in Figma waiting for implementation. A tighter design-engineering loop, even part-time, would have surfaced implementation constraints earlier and reduced rework on complex interactions like the matching game animations.

---

Lingawa taught me that product quality and team size don't correlate the way we assume. A solo designer with a design system, clear principles, and real user feedback can ship work that stands next to apps built by 50-person teams. The 5.0 ratings aren't a design award. They're proof that when the product does what users need, they notice.
